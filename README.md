# FINITE ELEMENTS APPLIED TO ENGINEERING – COURSE DESCRIPTION

The course Finite Elements Applied to Engineering belongs to the specialized curriculum area, is mandatory in nature, and has a theoretical–practical approach. Its main objective is to prepare students in the analysis and application of the Finite Element Method (FEM) for solving engineering problems, particularly in the fields of structural and mechanical analysis. The course develops the ability to formulate mathematical models, apply fundamental principles of mechanics, and use specialized computational tools to study the behavior of structural systems subjected to different loading conditions.

Throughout the course, students apply the concepts of elastic potential energy, variational principles, and the fundamental equations of the Theory of Elasticity to solve one-dimensional and two-dimensional design problems. Structural systems such as bars, trusses, beams, and frames are analyzed, including structures with non-articulated nodes, using the direct stiffness matrix method and the global analysis method. In addition, advanced numerical formulations based on the method of weighted residuals and the Galerkin method are introduced, as well as the isoparametric formulation of structural elements.

The course also covers the study of column buckling, transverse vibrations of beams, and two-dimensional elasticity, using triangular and rectangular elements for the modeling of continuous domains. To solve real engineering problems, specialized finite element analysis software is used, allowing students to integrate theory with practice and develop skills in the design, analysis, and simulation of structural systems.

The course content is organized into the following instructional units:

Unit I: Introduction, definitions, and fundamental operations. Global analysis method. Direct stiffness matrix method.
Unit II: Fundamental equations of the Theory of Elasticity. Variational principles of elastostatics. Principle of minimum potential energy. Principle of virtual work.
Unit III: Method of weighted residuals. Galerkin method. Isoparametric formulation of one-dimensional structural elements. Lagrangian elements.
Unit IV: Finite formulation of the column buckling problem. Finite formulation of transverse beam vibrations. Two-dimensional elasticity. Triangular and rectangular elements for the analysis of two-dimensional problems.
